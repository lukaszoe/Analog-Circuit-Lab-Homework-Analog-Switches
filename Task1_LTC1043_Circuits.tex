\section{LTC1043 Divider and Multiplier Circuits}

\subsection{Circuit Descriptions}

The LTC1043 is a monolithic switched-capacitor building block with dual switched-capacitor networks, an on-chip oscillator, and level translators. It does precision analog signal processing through charge-transfer techniques.

\subsubsection{Multiply-by-2 Circuit}

The multiply-by-2 circuit uses the LTC1043's two internal switched-capacitor networks to double the input voltage. On every clock half-cycle, the on-chip flying capacitors are reconfigured by the internal switches: during the sample phase, the on-chip flying cap is charged to $V_{\text{in}}$; during the transfer phase, it is switched in series with the external hold cap that already carries the previous output voltage, so the voltages add. With matched internal flying caps, the output converges to:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = 2 \cdot V_{\text{in}}
\end{equation}

The output is then buffered by an internal op-amp to provide a low-impedance output. (The exact implementation --- whether two on-chip flying caps are summed, or one is stacked onto the previous output --- is hidden inside the LTC1043 and is selected by how the external 0.1\,µF caps are wired to the S1A/S2A/S3A/S4A and Ca+/Ca- pins.)

\textbf{Circuit Analysis: Multiply-by-2 (1043-M2.asc).}
	Symmetric supply with $\pm 5\,\text{V}$ at the supply pins of the LTC1043, input $V_3 = 1\,\text{V}$.

	\textbf{Important clarification on the external capacitors.} $C_1 = 10\,\text{nF}$ is \emph{not} the flying capacitor --- it is the \textbf{oscillator timing capacitor} at the \texttt{Cosc} pin, which sets the internal clock frequency $f_{\text{clk}} \approx 150\,\text{kHz}$. The actual flying capacitors are \emph{integrated on-chip} in the LTC1043 and not visible in the schematic. The external $C_2$ and $C_3$ (each 0.1\,µF) are the \textbf{hold / output filter capacitors} at the output node and at the auxiliary \texttt{Ca+}/\texttt{Ca-} pins.

	\textbf{Sample/transfer (internally).} The LTC1043's two internal SC networks toggle on every clock half-cycle between two configurations --- which one is active is set by external wiring of $C_2$ and $C_3$ to the switch pins (S1A, S2A, S3A, S4A, Ca+, Ca-). In the M2 file, the two external 0.1\,µF caps are wired so that one is summed in series with the previous output, giving a per-cycle voltage addition $V_{\text{out}} \to 2 \cdot V_{\text{in}}$. In the D2 file, they are wired so that charge is shared in parallel, giving $V_{\text{out}} \to V_{\text{in}}/2$.

	Transient simulation over 30\,ms with startup: $V_{\text{out}}$ converges to 2\,V.

\subsubsection{Divide-by-2 Circuit}

The divide-by-2 circuit halves the input voltage using the same LTC1043 SC network as the multiplier --- only the external wiring of the 0.1\,µF caps differs. On every clock half-cycle the on-chip flying cap is recharged to $V_{\text{in}}$ and then connected in parallel with the external hold cap, redistributing the charge. With equal values, the output settles to:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = \frac{V_{\text{in}}}{2}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Circuit Analysis: Divide-by-2 (1043-D2.asc).}
	Same supply as M2. Input $V_3 = 1\,\text{V}$, oscillator cap $C_1 = 10\,\text{nF}$ (same role as in M2 --- sets $f_{\text{clk}}$, not a flying cap).

	\textbf{Sample/transfer (internally).} As in M2, the actual flying capacitors are integrated on-chip. The external wiring of $C_2$ (0.1\,µF, at the output/\texttt{Ca+} side) and $C_3$ (0.1\,µF, hold cap) connects the internal SC network so that during the transfer phase the on-chip flying cap shares its charge with the external $C_2$ in parallel --- effectively halving the input voltage at the output. This is the same M2 sample/transfer mechanism, only the pin-to-cap wiring of the external caps is swapped.

	$C_2$ (0.1\,µF) is the holding capacitor, $C_3$ (0.1\,µF) provides additional filtering. Transient over 3\,ms with startup: $V_{\text{out}} \to 0.5\,\text{V}$.

	Difference from M2: the external caps are wired to the LTC1043's switch pins in the parallel-charge-sharing configuration, instead of the series-addition configuration. The on-chip flying cap and clock behavior are identical.

\subsection{Influence of Operating Frequency and ON-Resistance}

\subsubsection{Operating Frequency}

The switching frequency $f_{\text{clk}}$ determines how often charge is transferred:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Higher frequency: more rapid charge transfer, less output ripple, faster settling. The effective resistance is $R_{\text{eff}} = 1 / (f_{\text{clk}} \cdot C)$ — higher frequency means lower equivalent resistance.
	\item Lower frequency: more drift between transfers, more ripple, slower settling. If $f_{\text{clk}}$ is too low relative to the signal bandwidth, the circuit can't track the input and distortion results.
\end{itemize}

In practice, $f_{\text{clk}}$ should be at least $50\times$ the signal bandwidth.

\textbf{Analysis: Impact of switching frequency and $R_{\text{on}}$.}
	Internal oscillator: $f_{\text{clk}} \approx 150\,\text{kHz}$ (with $C_{\text{osc}} = C_1 = 10\,\text{nF}$ at the \texttt{Cosc} pin). $R_{\text{on}}$ of the internal MOS switches is typically $\approx 300\,\Omega$.

	Time constant for charging the (internal) flying cap through the on-resistance: $\tau = R_{\text{on}} \cdot C_{\text{fly}}$. The internal flying cap is a few pF (on-chip), so $\tau$ is well below the 3.3\,µs half-period --- charge transfer is essentially complete. The 10\,nF external $C_1$ does \emph{not} sit in the signal path of the SC network, so its time constant with $R_{\text{on}}$ is irrelevant for accuracy.

	Ripple: the output ripple is set by the ratio of the (internal) flying cap to the external hold cap, i.e.\ $\Delta V \approx \frac{C_{\text{fly}}}{C_2}\cdot V_{\text{in}}$. With on-chip $C_{\text{fly}}$ in the pF range and external $C_2 = 0.1$\,µF, the ripple is well below 1\,mV per cycle, strongly filtered by the large hold caps. The earlier estimate of 100\,mV ripple was based on a misinterpretation of $C_1$ as the flying cap.

	Higher $f_{\text{clk}}$: less ripple, but higher current consumption ($\propto f_{\text{clk}}$). Higher $R_{\text{on}}$: only matters if it becomes comparable to the reciprocal of $C_{\text{fly}} \cdot f_{\text{clk}}$.

\subsubsection{ON-Resistance ($R_{\text{on}}$)}

The MOS switches inside the LTC1043 have finite ON-resistance $R_{\text{on}}$, which limits charge transfer:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The RC time constant $\tau = R_{\text{on}} \cdot C$ limits how fast the capacitor charges. If $\tau$ isn't much smaller than $T_{\text{clk}} / 2$, the capacitor doesn't fully charge, causing a gain error.
	\item The error is roughly $\propto \exp(-T_{\text{clk}} / (2\tau))$. Lower $R_{\text{on}}$ and higher $f_{\text{clk}}$ both improve accuracy as long as $\tau \ll T_{\text{clk}}/2$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Impact of Mismatched Capacitors ($C_2 \neq C_3$)}

For the LTC1043's internal SC network, the actual gain ratios are set by the on-chip flying-cap matching, not by the external $C_2$ and $C_3$. The external caps only act as hold / filter caps; their mismatch mainly affects residual ripple and settling time, not the DC ratio. The formulas below describe what \emph{would} happen if the external caps were the active divider/multiplier network (a useful intuition-building exercise) --- the real LTC1043, however, is far less sensitive to external-cap tolerance than these formulas suggest.

\subsubsection{Multiply-by-2 Circuit}
If the multiplier ratio were determined by external caps:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{C_2}{C_3}\right)
\end{equation}
A mismatch would cause a gain error.

\subsubsection{Divide-by-2 Circuit}
If the divider ratio were determined by external caps:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \frac{C_2}{C_2 + C_3}
\end{equation}
A mismatch would cause a division ratio error.

The LTC1043's 12\,bit ($\approx 0.01\,\%$) accuracy in practice comes from internal on-chip matching, not from external-cap selection.

\textbf{Analysis: Impact of capacitor mismatch ($C_2 \neq C_3$).}
	In the simulations, $C_2 = C_3 = 0.1\,\mu\text{F}$ (identical). Both caps are external hold/filter caps at the output side of the SC network --- mismatch between them primarily affects output filtering, not the gain. The actual gain accuracy of the LTC1043 divider/multiplier is dominated by the \emph{internal} on-chip flying-cap matching (which is excellent, typically $< 0.1\,\%$).

	\textbf{Theoretical impact of 10\,\% mismatch between external $C_2$ and $C_3$:} the steady-state DC gain is essentially unchanged (the LTC1043's internal network sets the ratio), but residual ripple and settling time degrade. The earlier error figures of $+5.6\,\%$ (M2) and $+5.3\,\%$ (D2) were derived under the wrong assumption that the external 0.1\,µF caps are the flying/divider caps; in reality the LTC1043 determines the ratio internally, so a 10\,\% external-cap mismatch does not produce a 5\,\% gain error.

	The datasheet's accuracy figure of 12\,bit ($\approx 0.01\,\%$) refers to the \emph{internal} flying-cap matching, which is preserved as long as the external caps are large enough to act as a low-impedance voltage source/sink at the output. On discrete prototypes with external 0.1\,µF ceramic caps, parasitic tolerance ($5$--$20\,\%$) matters mainly for ripple and settling, not for the gain itself.
