\section{LTC1043 Divider and Multiplier Circuits}

\subsection{Circuit Descriptions}

The LTC1043 is a monolithic switched-capacitor building block with dual switched-capacitor networks, an on-chip oscillator, and level translators. It does precision analog signal processing through charge-transfer techniques.

\subsubsection{Multiply-by-2 Circuit}

The multiply-by-2 circuit uses the LTC1043's switched-capacitor network to double the input voltage. During the sample phase, capacitor $C_2$ charges to $V_{\text{in}}$. During the transfer phase, $C_2$ is placed in series with $C_3$, which already holds the previous output voltage. With equal capacitor values, the output doubles:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = 2 \cdot V_{\text{in}}
\end{equation}

The op-amp at the output buffers the voltage and provides low-impedance output.

\textbf{Circuit Analysis: Multiply-by-2 (1043-M2.asc).}
	Symmetric supply with $\pm 5\,\text{V}$ at pins 15/12, input $V_3 = 1\,\text{V}$.

	The flying capacitor $C_1 = 10\,\text{nF}$ is switched each clock phase:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Sample phase: $C_1$ charges to $V_{\text{in}}$
		\item Transfer phase: $C_1$ in series with $C_3$ (0.1\,µF) --- voltages add up, $V_{\text{out}} \to 2 \cdot V_{\text{in}}$
	\end{itemize}

	$C_2$ and $C_3$ (each 0.1\,µF) are 10$\times$ larger than $C_1$ and act as holding capacitors --- low ripple, smoother output. Transient simulation over 30\,ms with startup: $V_{\text{out}}$ converges to 2\,V.

\subsubsection{Divide-by-2 Circuit}

The divide-by-2 circuit halves the input voltage. During the sample phase, $C_2$ charges to $V_{\text{in}}$. During the transfer phase, $C_2$ connects in parallel with $C_3$, redistributing the charge. With $C_2 = C_3$:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = \frac{V_{\text{in}}}{2}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Circuit Analysis: Divide-by-2 (1043-D2.asc).}
	Same supply as M2. Input $V_3 = 1\,\text{V}$, flying capacitor $C_1 = 10\,\text{nF}$.

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Sample phase: $C_1$ charges to $V_{\text{in}}$
		\item Transfer phase: $C_1$ in parallel with $C_2$ (0.1\,µF) --- charge is shared, $V_{\text{out}} \to V_{\text{in}}/2$
	\end{itemize}

	$C_2$ (0.1\,µF) is the holding capacitor, $C_3$ (0.1\,µF) provides additional filtering. Transient over 3\,ms with startup: $V_{\text{out}} \to 0.5\,\text{V}$.

	Difference from M2: in the multiplier, $C_1$ is connected \emph{in series} (voltages add up); in the divider, \emph{in parallel} (charge is shared).

\subsection{Influence of Operating Frequency and ON-Resistance}

\subsubsection{Operating Frequency}

The switching frequency $f_{\text{clk}}$ determines how often charge is transferred:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Higher frequency: more rapid charge transfer, less output ripple, faster settling. The effective resistance is $R_{\text{eff}} = 1 / (f_{\text{clk}} \cdot C)$ — higher frequency means lower equivalent resistance.
	\item Lower frequency: more drift between transfers, more ripple, slower settling. If $f_{\text{clk}}$ is too low relative to the signal bandwidth, the circuit can't track the input and distortion results.
\end{itemize}

In practice, $f_{\text{clk}}$ should be at least $50\times$ the signal bandwidth.

\textbf{Analysis: Impact of switching frequency and $R_{\text{on}}$.}
	Internal oscillator: $f_{\text{clk}} \approx 150\,\text{kHz}$ (with $C_{\text{osc}} = 10\,\text{nF}$). $R_{\text{on}}$ typically $\approx 300\,\Omega$.

	Time constant: $\tau = R_{\text{on}} \cdot C_1 = 300\,\Omega \cdot 10\,\text{nF} = 3\,\mu\text{s}$. Half-period at 150\,kHz: $T_{\text{clk}}/2 \approx 3.3\,\mu\text{s}$. Ratio $T/(2\tau) \approx 1.1$ --- the capacitor charges to only about 67\,\% per half-phase. In practice, the duty cycle of the internal oscillator compensates for this, and the effective time constant is smaller (lower impedance from the output driver).

	Ripple: $\Delta V \approx \frac{C_1}{C_{\text{hold}}} \cdot V_{\text{in}} = \frac{10\,\text{nF}}{100\,\text{nF}} \cdot 1\,\text{V} \approx 100\,\text{mV}$ per cycle, strongly reduced over many cycles.

	Higher frequency: less ripple, but higher current consumption ($\propto f_{\text{clk}}$). Higher $R_{\text{on}}$: larger time constant, more incomplete charging, error increases exponentially.

\subsubsection{ON-Resistance ($R_{\text{on}}$)}

The MOS switches inside the LTC1043 have finite ON-resistance $R_{\text{on}}$, which limits charge transfer:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The RC time constant $\tau = R_{\text{on}} \cdot C$ limits how fast the capacitor charges. If $\tau$ isn't much smaller than $T_{\text{clk}} / 2$, the capacitor doesn't fully charge, causing a gain error.
	\item The error is roughly $\propto \exp(-T_{\text{clk}} / (2\tau))$. Lower $R_{\text{on}}$ and higher $f_{\text{clk}}$ both improve accuracy as long as $\tau \ll T_{\text{clk}}/2$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Impact of Mismatched Capacitors ($C_2 \neq C_3$)}

When $C_2$ and $C_3$ aren't identical, the charge redistribution is no longer symmetrical.

\subsubsection{Multiply-by-2 Circuit}
With $C_2 \neq C_3$:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{C_2}{C_3}\right)
\end{equation}
If $C_2 = C_3$, this reduces to $2 \cdot V_{\text{in}}$. A mismatch causes a gain error.

\subsubsection{Divide-by-2 Circuit}
With $C_2 \neq C_3$:
\begin{equation}
	V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \frac{C_2}{C_2 + C_3}
\end{equation}
Only when $C_2 = C_3$ does this yield $V_{\text{in}} / 2$. Mismatch directly causes a division ratio error.

In both cases, the gain error is proportional to $\Delta C / C$. Precision applications need matched capacitors (e.g., \SI{0.01}{\percent} for \SI{12}{bit} accuracy) or trimming.

\textbf{Analysis: Impact of capacitor mismatch ($C_2 \neq C_3$).}
	In the simulations, $C_2 = C_3 = 0.1\,\mu\text{F}$ (identical). Theoretical impact at 10\,\% deviation ($C_3 = 0.09\,\mu\text{F}$):

	M2: $V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot (1 + 1.\overline{1}) = 2.\overline{1} \cdot V_{\text{in}}$ $\to$ error $+5.6\,\%$.

	D2: $V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \frac{0.1}{0.19} \approx 0.526 \cdot V_{\text{in}}$ $\to$ error $+5.3\,\%$.

	The flying capacitor $C_1 = 10\,\text{nF}$ determines the charge transferred per cycle, but $C_2$/$C_3$ determine the steady-state output voltage. Mismatch $C_1$ vs.\ $C_2/C_3$ $\to$ slower settling. Mismatch $C_2$ vs.\ $C_3$ $\to$ direct accuracy error.

	On ICs: $< 0.1\,\%$ matching is achievable (10-bit suffices). Discrete: 5--20\,\% tolerance for ceramic capacitors --- precision requires trimming or selection.
