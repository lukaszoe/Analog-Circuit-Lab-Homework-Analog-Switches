\section{LTC1043 Applications}

The following circuits are from the LTC1043 datasheet (Applications section). Each uses the LTC1043's precision switched-capacitor technique for a specific analog signal processing function.

\subsection{Instrumentation Amplifier}

The LTC1043-based instrumentation amp achieves high CMRR without tightly matched resistors. The LTC1043's switched-capacitor network samples the differential input $V_{\text{in+}} - V_{\text{in-}}$ during one clock phase and transfers this charge to a single-ended output in the next phase. Because the charge transfer relies on capacitor ratios (which can be made very precise on-chip), CMRR depends on capacitor matching, not resistor matching. A single op-amp at the output amplifies the transferred voltage to the desired gain.

\textbf{Key advantage:} CMRR is limited by capacitor matching, not resistor matching — values exceeding \SI{100}{\decibel} are possible without laser-trimmed resistors. Input impedance is also very high (set by capacitor leakage, not resistor values), so the signal source isn't loaded.

\subsection{Voltage-Controlled Current Source}

The LTC1043 can implement a precision VCCS. The input voltage converts to a proportional current through a switched-capacitor resistor equivalent: $R_{\text{eff}} = 1 / (f_{\text{clk}} \cdot C)$. The flying capacitor samples the control voltage and transfers charge to a sense resistor. An op-amp in feedback ensures the voltage across the sense resistor equals the input voltage, giving $I_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} / R_{\text{sense}}$.

\textbf{Key advantage:} Output current accuracy depends on capacitor matching and clock frequency stability rather than resistor accuracy — suitable for precision current sourcing in sensor excitation and 4--20\,mA loops.

\subsection{Frequency-to-Voltage Converter}

The LTC1043 converts an input frequency to a proportional output voltage. The input frequency drives the clock input — each pulse triggers one charge-transfer cycle. A fixed capacitor $C_{\text{ref}}$ charges to $V_{\text{ref}}$ per cycle, and this charge transfers to $C_{\text{out}}$. The average current is $I_{\text{avg}} = V_{\text{ref}} \cdot C_{\text{ref}} \cdot f_{\text{in}}$, and a load resistor converts it: $V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{ref}} \cdot C_{\text{ref}} \cdot f_{\text{in}} \cdot R_{\text{load}}$.

\textbf{Key advantage:} The conversion is inherently linear because each cycle transfers a fixed charge quantum. Linearity depends on the stability of $V_{\text{ref}}$, $C_{\text{ref}}$, and charge-transfer completeness — not on the linearity of any active device. An RC low-pass at the output smooths the ripple.

\section{Additional Circuit Analyses (Visual Inspection)}

The following circuits are from \texttt{Diode-Quartet.asc} and \texttt{Switched-C.asc}. They do not use the LTC1043, but show other aspects of analog switches and diodes. Analysis by visual inspection of the schematics, as recommended by the lab instructor.

\subsection{Diode-Quartet (Diode-Quartet.asc)}

\subsubsection{Topology and Components}

The file contains six diodes (D1--D6), a $\pm 5\,\text{V}$ supply, an AC source $V_3 = \text{SINE}(0,\,1,\,1\,\text{MHz})$, two 50\,$\Omega$ resistors, two 10\,mA current sources, and two additional 5\,V sources for clamping.

\subsubsection{Circuit Analysis}

D1 through D4 form a classic full-wave bridge rectifier. During the positive half-cycle of the input, D1 and D2 conduct while D3 and D4 block; in the negative half-cycle, D3 and D4 take over. Either way, current flows through the load in the same direction. The 50\,$\Omega$ resistors on input and output are typical source and load impedances for a 50\,$\Omega$ system.

D5 and D6 sit in parallel with the bridge and act as clippers together with the auxiliary 5\,V sources. The output is therefore clamped to roughly $\pm 5.7\,\text{V}$, protecting whatever follows the rectifier.

The 10\,mA current sources most likely provide bias for the diodes, which makes the forward-voltage drop more predictable than without bias. At 1\,MHz, real silicon diodes would struggle; Schottky diodes would be the better choice for this frequency.

The simulation runs a Fourier analysis at 1\,MHz with ten harmonics, which would let one determine the THD of the rectifier. An ideal full-wave rectifier should produce only even harmonics.

\subsection{Switched Capacitor (Switched-C.asc)}

\subsubsection{Topology and Components}

The file contains a step-voltage source $V_1$ that switches from $-1$ to $+1\,\text{V}$ at $t = 1\,\text{ms}$, an ideal voltage-controlled switch S1, a control source $V_2$ that ramps from 1 to 4\,V, a 1\,µF capacitor $C_2$, and a 100\,$\Omega$ series resistor $R_1$.

\subsubsection{Circuit Analysis}

When the switch is closed, $C_2$ charges towards $V_1$ through $R_1$ with a time constant of 100\,µs. After about five time constants, $C_2$ has settled to over 99\,\% of $V_1$. When the switch opens, the charge on $C_2$ is held --- the output keeps its last value until the switch closes again.

This is the same basic principle the LTC1043 uses, just stripped down to one switch and one capacitor. The LTC1043 toggles two internal flying capacitors with its own oscillator; here the switch is controlled manually by $V_2$ and only one capacitor is involved.

If the switch were toggled periodically at some clock rate $f_{\text{clk}}$, the network would behave like an equivalent resistor of $R_{\text{eq}} = 1 / (f_{\text{clk}} \cdot C_2)$. For the values in the schematic, $R_{\text{eq}}$ would equal $R_1 = 100\,\Omega$ at roughly 10\,kHz, which is the classic switched-capacitor resistor equivalence.

The slow ramp of $V_2$ from 1 to 4\,V suggests the original author wanted to test whether the switch's on-resistance depends on its control voltage --- something a real MOSFET would show, but not the ideal model used here.

\subsection{Notes on Interpreting the Triangle Symbols}

The lab instructor pointed out that the triangle symbol could be an amplifier or a comparator. In LTspice, both look identical.

The way to tell them apart is by the surrounding circuit. An op-amp always has negative feedback --- the output is wired back to the inverting input --- and the output is an analog voltage. A comparator has no such feedback, and its output swings between the supply rails as a digital signal.

In the four files considered here, only the LTC1043 schematics contain a triangle symbol (inside the IC block), and those are clearly amplifiers since the output is an analog voltage. The other two schematics use only discrete components, so the question doesn't arise.
