\section{LTC1043 Applications}

The following circuits are from the LTC1043 datasheet (Applications section). Each uses the LTC1043's precision switched-capacitor technique for a specific analog signal processing function.

\subsection{Instrumentation Amplifier}

The LTC1043-based instrumentation amp achieves high CMRR without tightly matched resistors. The LTC1043's switched-capacitor network samples the differential input $V_{\text{in+}} - V_{\text{in-}}$ during one clock phase and transfers this charge to a single-ended output in the next phase. Because the charge transfer relies on capacitor ratios (which can be made very precise on-chip), CMRR depends on capacitor matching, not resistor matching. A single op-amp at the output amplifies the transferred voltage to the desired gain.

\textbf{Key advantage:} CMRR is limited by capacitor matching, not resistor matching — values exceeding \SI{100}{\decibel} are possible without laser-trimmed resistors. Input impedance is also very high (set by capacitor leakage, not resistor values), so the signal source isn't loaded.

\subsection{Voltage-Controlled Current Source}

The LTC1043 can implement a precision VCCS. The input voltage converts to a proportional current through a switched-capacitor resistor equivalent: $R_{\text{eff}} = 1 / (f_{\text{clk}} \cdot C)$. The flying capacitor samples the control voltage and transfers charge to a sense resistor. An op-amp in feedback ensures the voltage across the sense resistor equals the input voltage, giving $I_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} / R_{\text{sense}}$.

\textbf{Key advantage:} Output current accuracy depends on capacitor matching and clock frequency stability rather than resistor accuracy — suitable for precision current sourcing in sensor excitation and 4--20\,mA loops.

\subsection{Frequency-to-Voltage Converter}

The LTC1043 converts an input frequency to a proportional output voltage. The input frequency drives the clock input — each pulse triggers one charge-transfer cycle. A fixed capacitor $C_{\text{ref}}$ charges to $V_{\text{ref}}$ per cycle, and this charge transfers to $C_{\text{out}}$. The average current is $I_{\text{avg}} = V_{\text{ref}} \cdot C_{\text{ref}} \cdot f_{\text{in}}$, and a load resistor converts it: $V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{ref}} \cdot C_{\text{ref}} \cdot f_{\text{in}} \cdot R_{\text{load}}$.

\textbf{Key advantage:} The conversion is inherently linear because each cycle transfers a fixed charge quantum. Linearity depends on the stability of $V_{\text{ref}}$, $C_{\text{ref}}$, and charge-transfer completeness — not on the linearity of any active device. An RC low-pass at the output smooths the ripple.

\section{Additional Circuit Analyses (Visual Inspection)}

The following circuits are from the LTspice files \texttt{Diode-Quartet.asc} and \texttt{Switched-C.asc}. They do not contain an LTC1043, but demonstrate other aspects of analog switches and diodes. Analysis by \emph{visual inspection} of the schematics, as recommended by the lab instructor.

\subsection{Diode-Quartet (Diode-Quartet.asc)}

\subsubsection{Topology and Components}

Six diodes (D1--D6), $\pm 5\,\text{V}$ supply, AC voltage $V_3 = \text{SINE}(0,\,1,\,1\,\text{MHz})$ with AC\,1, $R_1 = R_2 = 50\,\Omega$, current sources $I_1 = I_2 = 10\,\text{mA}$, and $V_6 = +5\,\text{V}$, $V_7 = -5\,\text{V}$.

\subsubsection{Circuit Analysis}

\textbf{Diode bridge (D1--D4):} Full-wave bridge rectifier (Graetz circuit). Positive half-cycle: D1/D2 conduct, D3/D4 block. Negative half-cycle: D3/D4 conduct, D1/D2 block. Result: rectified current through the load, independent of input polarity.

\textbf{Input:} $V_3$ provides 1\,V at 1\,MHz. $R_1 = 50\,\Omega$ --- typical 50\,$\Omega$ source impedance, limits input current.

\textbf{Output:} $R_2 = 50\,\Omega$ at node \texttt{V\_out}. Rectified envelope minus $\approx 2 \times 0.7\,\text{V}$ diode drop per half-cycle.

\textbf{D5 and D6:} Together with $V_6 = +5\,\text{V}$ and $V_7 = -5\,\text{V}$ they act as clippers: D5 conducts when $V_{\text{out}} > +5\,\text{V} - V_D$, D6 when $V_{\text{out}} < -5\,\text{V} + V_D$. Output is clamped to approximately $[-5.7\,\text{V},\,+5.7\,\text{V}]$.

\textbf{Current sources $I_1$ and $I_2$:} Each provides 10\,mA DC --- likely bias current for the diodes, making the voltage drop better defined.

\textbf{Fourier analysis:} \texttt{.four 1meg 10 -1 V(n004) V(V\_in) V(V\_out)} --- THD determination of the rectifier. An ideal full-wave rectifier produces only even harmonics (2f, 4f, \ldots).

Full-wave rectifier with 50\,$\Omega$ matching and clipper protection. At 1\,MHz, Schottky diodes would be ideal (low forward voltage, fast recovery).

\subsection{Switched Capacitor (Switched-C.asc)}

\subsubsection{Topology and Components}

\begin{itemize}
\item $V_1$: \texttt{PWL(0 -1 1m -1 1.001m 1)} --- step from $-1\,\text{V}$ to $+1\,\text{V}$ at $t = 1\,\text{ms}$
\item S1: Voltage-controlled switch (\texttt{.model sw sw()}) --- ideal, 0/1 threshold
\item $V_2$: \texttt{PWL(0 1 2m 1 5m 4)} --- control voltage, $>1\,\text{V}$ = switch closed
\item $C_2 = 1\,\mu\text{F}$, IC=0; $R_1 = 100\,\Omega$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Circuit Analysis}

\textbf{Operation:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Switch closed: $V_1$ connected to $C_2$ via $R_1$. $\tau = R_1 \cdot C_2 = 100\,\mu\text{s}$. After $5\tau = 500\,\mu\text{s}$, $C_2$ is charged to over 99\,\%.
\item Switch open: charge on $C_2$ is preserved, $V_{\text{out}}$ holds the last value.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Signal flow:} $t < 1\,\text{ms}$: $V_1 = -1\,\text{V}$, switch closed, $C_2 \to -1\,\text{V}$. At $t = 1\,\text{ms}$: $V_1$ steps to $+1\,\text{V}$, $C_2$ recharges (RC delay). Until $t = 5\,\text{ms}$: switch remains closed, $V_2$ slowly rises from 1 to 4\,V (does not affect switch state).

\textbf{Relation to the LTC1043:} Same basic idea --- except the LTC1043 switches automatically with its oscillator and alternates between two capacitors (flying capacitor). Here: single switch, single capacitor, isolated principle.

\textbf{Equivalent resistance:} With periodic switching at $f_{\text{clk}}$: $R_{\text{eq}} = 1 / (f_{\text{clk}} \cdot C_2)$. With $C_2 = 1\,\mu\text{F}$ and $f_{\text{clk}} = 10\,\text{kHz}$, $R_{\text{eq}} = 100\,\Omega$ --- exactly $R_1$. This is how a switched capacitor can replace a physical resistor.

\textbf{Note on control voltage:} $V_2$ ramps from 1 to 4\,V instead of switching --- presumably a test of whether the switch shows different $R_{\text{on}}$ at different control voltages (for real MOS switches: higher $V_{\text{GS}}$ $\to$ lower $R_{\text{on}}$). The \texttt{sw sw()} model is ideal, however, so there is no effect in this simulation.

\subsection{Notes on Interpreting the Triangle Symbols}

The lab instructor suggests looking at the triangle symbol --- amplifier or comparator?

In LTspice, both look identical. The difference:
\begin{itemize}
\item Op-amp: has negative feedback (output connected to inverting input), output is analog/linear
\item Comparator: no negative feedback, output is digital (switches to $V^+$ or $V^-$)
\end{itemize}

In the circuits under consideration: \texttt{1043-D2.asc} and \texttt{1043-M2.asc} have analog outputs $\to$ amplifiers. \texttt{Diode-Quartet.asc} and \texttt{Switched-C.asc} contain no triangle symbol.

Rule of thumb: negative feedback $\to$ op-amp. No negative feedback $\to$ likely a comparator. An op-amp without negative feedback can work as a comparator (but slowly); a comparator with negative feedback oscillates.
